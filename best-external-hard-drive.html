Why You Need an External Hard Drive

Laptop hard drives fail. Cloud storage has monthly fees and privacy concerns. An external hard drive gives you a physical backup of your most important files that you control completely — photos, business documents, client work, creative projects, financial records. Losing this data without a backup can be catastrophic. An external hard drive is the simplest and most reliable insurance against data loss.

For content creators, freelancers and online business owners who accumulate large amounts of files, an external hard drive also solves the storage problem — keeping your laptop free and fast while all your archive files live on the external drive.

HDD vs SSD — Which Should You Choose?

Choose HDD (like WD My Passport) if you want maximum storage capacity at minimum cost. 2TB to 5TB at affordable prices. Best for backup and archive storage where transfer speed is not critical.

Choose SSD (like Samsung T7) if you need fast transfer speeds — working directly from the drive with video files, large databases or running applications from the drive. SSDs are also more durable as they have no moving parts to damage if dropped.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much storage do I need on an external hard drive?
For most people 1TB to 2TB is more than enough for years of documents, photos and backups. Content creators working with video should consider 4TB or larger. As a rough guide — 1TB stores approximately 250,000 photos or 500 hours of video at standard quality.
How long do external hard drives last?
Traditional HDDs typically last 3 to 5 years with regular use. SSDs generally last longer as they have no moving parts. Regardless of type it is important not to rely on a single external drive as your only backup — use the 3-2-1 rule: 3 copies of your data, 2 different storage types, 1 offsite backup.
Can I use an external hard drive with both Mac and Windows?
Yes — both the WD My Passport and Samsung T7 work with Mac and Windows. They may need reformatting when first used with a Mac if they came pre-formatted for Windows. The WD and Samsung software handles this automatically on first connection.
